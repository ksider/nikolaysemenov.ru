PhD Dissertation · 2013

Rheology of electrically controlled nanosuspensions

Photo / video placeholder — a rheometer flow curve or lab photo fits here.

Context

Some suspensions of nanoscale particles can shift from behaving like a liquid to behaving like a near-solid in milliseconds, simply by switching on an electric field — a property called the electrorheological effect.

Approach

The dissertation examined how field strength, particle concentration, and flow conditions govern that transition in polyimide-based nanosuspensions, using rotational and dynamic rheometry to map the material's behavior under shear.

Why it stuck around

That same instinct — for how a material's internal structure governs its flow and processing behavior — carried forward into every role since, from stabilizing moisture-sensitive biopolymers to tuning field-responsive elastomers.
